\subsection{Markets and sample}
\label{sec:data:markets}

The dataset consists of 1-minute resolution day-session price histories for
eight equity-index futures contracts spanning four geographical regions. The
data were sourced from OneTick via TickData and cover each contract from its
exchange inception through 14 January 2026. Table~\ref{tab:markets} provides
a full description of each contract.

The eight contracts were selected to provide broad coverage across asset
classes and geographies rather than on the basis of any statistical
pre-screening. Three North American equity-index contracts (YM, NQ, ER, ES)
trade on CME Group venues; one Canadian contract (PT) trades on the Montreal
Stock Exchange; and three European contracts (FT, DA, CF) cover the major
continental and UK equity benchmarks.

The raw files are stored in ``skip'' format: minutes with no transactions are
omitted from the file rather than recorded as zero-volume observations. This
is addressed in preprocessing (Section~\ref{sec:data:preprocessing}).

To justify the use of 1-minute price changes as the elementary increment,
Table~\ref{tab:sigma} reports the standard deviation of 1-minute price
changes $\sigma_1$ and the average bid-ask spread $\Delta$ for each market.
A necessary condition for 1-minute returns to carry genuine price information
is $\sigma_1 / \Delta > 1$; otherwise a substantial fraction of observed
changes reflects the bid-ask bounce rather than true price movement
\parencite{Roll1984}. As the ratio $\sigma_1/\Delta$ exceeds 1 for all eight
markets, the 1-minute frequency is appropriate.

\subsection{Preprocessing}
\label{sec:data:preprocessing}

Three preprocessing steps are applied uniformly across all markets before any
statistical analysis.

\textbf{Integer-scale conversion.} Raw prices are quoted in their native tick
units. To work in integer arithmetic and avoid floating-point accumulation
errors, each price series is multiplied by the contract-specific scaling
factor $1/\Delta$ and rounded to the nearest integer, so that one tick
corresponds to one integer unit. All subsequent price differences are
therefore integer-valued.

\textbf{Gap-filling.} The raw files omit minutes with no transactions. Missing
minutes within a trading session are filled by carrying forward the last
observed price (last-observation-carried-forward, LOCF). This preserves the
equal-spacing of the 1-minute grid required for the lag-$\tau$ increment
construction while recording zero price change during illiquid periods, which
is consistent with the true mid-price behavior in the absence of transactions.

\textbf{Lag grid construction.} Price increments $\delta_\tau(t) = P(t) -
P(t-\tau)$ are formed for each lag $\tau$ on two log-spaced grids. The coarse
grid ($N_\tau = 20$) spans $\tau \in \{1, 2, 3, 6, \ldots, 56{,}234\}$
minutes; the fine grid ($N_\tau = 37$) spans the same range with higher
density. The maximum lag of 56,234 minutes corresponds to approximately six
months of trading for a 405-minute session, well beyond the scale at which
convergence to Gaussian behavior is expected. Both grids are used in all
estimations to assess the sensitivity of $\alphah$ to the choice of $N_\tau$.
